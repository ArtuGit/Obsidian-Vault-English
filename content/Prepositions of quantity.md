
Prepositions of quantity are important when describing **how much** or **how many** of something there is. They often appear with **"of"** constructions (like "most of", "none of", etc.), and are closely tied to whether the noun is **countable** or **uncountable**.

### **Of**

Used to show a part or amount of something.  
**Examples:**

- A piece **of** cake
    
- A bottle **of** water
    
- A lot **of** people
    
- Most **of** the time
    
- None **of** them
    

### **Some**

Indicates an unspecified quantity, more than one but not all.  
**Examples:**

- I need **some** sugar.
    
- **Some** of the students are absent.
    

### **Many**

Used with countable nouns to indicate a large number.  
**Examples:**

- **Many** people attended the event.
    
- **Many** of the apples were rotten.
    

### **Much**

Used with uncountable nouns to indicate a large quantity.  
**Examples:**

- He doesn’t have **much** money.
    
- **Much** of the work is done.
    

### **Any**

Used in negative or interrogative sentences to refer to an unspecified quantity.  
**Examples:**

- Do you have **any** questions?
    
- I don’t have **any** time.
    

### **Few / A Few**

Used with countable nouns to refer to a small number.  
**Examples:**

- **Few** people came to the meeting (almost none).
    
- **A few** people came to the meeting (some did).
    

### **Little / A Little**

Used with uncountable nouns to refer to a small amount.  
**Examples:**

- **Little** water was left (almost none).
    
- **A little** water was left (some was left).
    

### **All**

Refers to the whole quantity or number.  
**Examples:**

- **All** the students passed the test.
    
- She drank **all** of the milk.
    

### **Half**

Indicates 50% of something.  
**Examples:**

- **Half** of the class is absent.
    
- I ate **half** the cake.
    

### **Most**

Indicates a majority.  
**Examples:**

- **Most** people like chocolate.
    
- **Most** of the money was spent.
    

### **None**

Indicates zero quantity.  
**Examples:**

- **None** of the students knew the answer.
    
- **None** were left.
    

### **Each / Every**

Refers to individuals within a group.  
**Examples:**

- **Each** student has a book.
    
- **Every** bottle was sealed.
