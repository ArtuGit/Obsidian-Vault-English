The Present Perfect Continuous emphasizes the duration or ongoing nature of an action that started in the past and continues to the present (or has just stopped with visible results). Compare with [[Present Perfect]] which focuses on completion or result.

---

## Form

### ✅ Positive (Affirmative)

| Subject | Structure | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| I / You / We / They | have been + verb-**ing** | I **have been waiting** for an hour. |
| He / She / It | has been + verb-**ing** | She **has been working** all day. |

### ❌ Negative

| Subject | Structure | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| I / You / We / They | have not (haven't) been + verb-ing | I **haven't been sleeping** well. |
| He / She / It | has not (hasn't) been + verb-ing | He **hasn't been feeling** well. |

### ❓ Questions

| Type | Structure | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Yes/No | Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing? | **Have** you **been waiting** long? |
| Wh- | Wh-word + have/has + subject + been + verb-ing? | **What have** you **been doing**? |

**Asking about duration:**
- **How long have** you **been learning** English?

### Short Answers

| Question | Yes | No |
|----------|-----|-----|
| Have you been running? | Yes, I **have**. | No, I **haven't**. |
| Has she been crying? | Yes, she **has**. | No, she **hasn't**. |

---

## Uses

### 1. Duration of Activity Up to Now
Emphasizing how long an activity has been in progress.

- I **have been studying** English for three years.
- She **has been working** here since 2018.
- We **have been waiting** for over an hour.

### 2. Recent Activity with Visible Results
An action that recently stopped, and you can see the evidence.

- You're sweating. **Have** you **been running**?
- Her eyes are red. She **has been crying**.
- The ground is wet. It **has been raining**.

### 3. Temporary Ongoing Situations
Situations that have been happening recently and may continue.

- I **have been thinking** about changing jobs.
- He **has been living** with his parents lately.
- We **have been having** problems with our internet.

### 4. Repeated Actions Over a Period
Actions happening repeatedly from past to present.

- She **has been calling** me every day this week.
- I **have been going** to the gym regularly.
- They **have been meeting** secretly.

### 5. Explaining a Current Situation
Why something is the way it is now.

- I'm tired because I **have been working** all day.
- My hands are dirty because I **have been gardening**.
- Sorry I'm late—I **have been dealing** with an emergency.

## Signal Words / Time Markers

| Duration Words | Time Expressions |
|----------------|------------------|
| for (+ period) | all day / all morning |
| since (+ point in time) | all week / all month |
| How long...? | lately / recently |
| the whole day | these days |
| | this week / this month |

## Common Mistakes

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Explanation |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| I am working here for 5 years. | I **have been working** here for 5 years. | Duration to now needs Perfect Continuous |
| She has been knowing him for years. | She **has known** him for years. | *Know* is stative—no continuous |
| I have been finishing the project. | I **have finished** the project. | Completed actions use Present Perfect |
| How long do you wait? | How long **have** you **been waiting**? | Duration to now = Perfect Continuous |
| He has being working hard. | He **has been** working hard. | *Been*, not *being* |
| I have been living here since 3 years. | I have been living here **for** 3 years. | *For* + period, *since* + point |

## Tense Comparison: Present Perfect Continuous vs. Present Perfect

| Present Perfect Continuous | Present Perfect |
|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Emphasizes **duration/process** | Emphasizes **result/completion** |
| The activity may be **unfinished** | The activity is often **complete** |
| Focus on **how long** | Focus on **how many/much** |
| Often with **for/since** | Often with **already/yet/just** |

**Examples:**

| Present Perfect Continuous | Present Perfect |
|---------------------------|-----------------|
| I **have been reading** this book. *(still reading, focus on process)* | I **have read** this book. *(finished, focus on result)* |
| She **has been writing** emails. *(focus on activity)* | She **has written** 10 emails. *(focus on quantity)* |
| It **has been snowing**. *(focus on duration)* | It **has snowed** a lot. *(focus on amount)* |
| I **have been learning** English for 2 years. *(ongoing)* | I **have learned** a lot of English. *(achievement)* |

**Duration comparison:**
- I **have been reading** for two hours. *(emphasizes the ongoing process)*
- I **have read** for two hours. *(acceptable but less common—less emphasis on duration)*

## Stative Verbs (No Continuous Form)

These verbs don't take the continuous form—use [[Present Perfect]] instead:

| Category | Use Present Perfect |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Mental states** | I **have known** him for years. *(not: have been knowing)* |
| **Emotions** | She **has loved** music since childhood. |
| **Possession** | We **have had** this car for 10 years. |
| **Senses** | I **have always believed** in you. |

---

## Present Perfect Continuous vs. Present Continuous

| Present Perfect Continuous | Present Continuous |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Started in past, continues to now | Happening right now |
| Emphasizes duration | Emphasizes the moment |
| "How long have you been...?" | "What are you doing now?" |

**Examples:**
- I **have been working** on this project for 3 months. *(duration)*
- I **am working** on this project right now. *(current action)*

## Passive Voice

⚠️ **The Present Perfect Continuous Passive is grammatically possible but rarely used.** It's awkward and usually avoided in favor of other constructions.

### Form

| Type | Structure | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Positive** | have/has + been + being + past participle | The house **has been being renovated**. |

### Why It's Rarely Used

The structure "has been being" is considered clumsy and overly complex. Native speakers typically prefer:

| Instead of (awkward) | Use (natural) |
|---------------------|---------------|
| The road **has been being repaired** for weeks. | The road **has been under repair** for weeks. |
| The issue **has been being discussed** all day. | The issue **has been discussed** all day. *(Present Perfect Passive)* |
| The project **has been being developed**. | They **have been developing** the project. *(Active voice)* |

### When You Might See It

Very rarely in formal or technical writing when emphasizing ongoing passive action:
- The matter **has been being investigated** since January.

**Recommendation:** Avoid this construction. Use [[Present Perfect]] Passive or rewrite in active voice.

---

## Practice Tips

1. **Explain visible evidence:** Look at situations and explain them. "The kitchen is messy—someone has been cooking. The grass is short—someone has been mowing."

2. **Duration questions:** Ask yourself "How long have I been...?" about current activities. "How long have I been living here? I've been living here for 5 years."

3. **Process vs. result:** Practice both tenses for the same topic. "I've been writing my thesis (process). I've written three chapters (result)."
