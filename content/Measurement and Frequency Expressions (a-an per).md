English often uses **a/an** to mean **“per”** in measurements and frequency expressions.

Examples:
- twice **a** week
- £10 **a** kilo
- 60 miles **an** hour

British English is the default in this vault. When American usage differs, see “American version”.

---

## B1/B2 core patterns

### Frequency
- once a day
- twice a week
- three times a month

### Price/quantity
- £10 a kilo
- £2 a litre

### Speed
- 60 miles an hour

---

## a/an vs per
Both can be correct; **a/an** is extremely common.
- £10 a kilo = £10 per kilo

---

## Common mistakes

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Why |
|---|---|---|
| twice Ø week | twice **a** week | fixed pattern |
| 60 miles a hour | 60 miles **an** hour | vowel sound |
| £10 the kilo | £10 **a** kilo | “per” meaning |

---

## Practice

### A) Fill the gaps with a/an
1) I go to the gym twice ___ week.
2) The train travels at 120 kilometres ___ hour.
3) Eggs cost £3 ___ dozen.
4) I pay £2 ___ litre.

### B) Rewrite using “per”
5) It costs £15 a kilo.
6) She visits her parents once a month.

---

## Answer key

1) a  
2) an  
3) a  
4) a  

5) It costs £15 per kilo.  
6) She visits her parents once per month. *(also common: once a month)*

---

## C1+ Notes

### “Per” is more formal
Per is common in formal writing, signs, and product labels.

---

## American version
Mostly the same. Units/spelling differ (miles vs kilometres) but the grammar pattern is identical.
