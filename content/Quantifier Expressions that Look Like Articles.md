Some expressions look like articles because they include **a/an** or behave like determiners.

This page covers:
- a few / few
- a little / little
- such a
- quite a
- what a

British English is the default in this vault. When American usage differs, see “American version”.

---

## a few vs few (countable)

### a few = some (positive)
- I have **a few** friends in London. *(some; enough)*

### few = not many (often negative)
- I have **few** friends here. *(not many; maybe not enough)*

---

## a little vs little (uncountable)

### a little = some (positive)
- There’s **a little** time. *(some time)*

### little = not much (often negative)
- There’s **little** time. *(almost none)*

---

## such a / what a / quite a

### such a + singular count noun
- She’s **such a** good teacher.

### what a + singular count noun (exclamation)
- **What a** surprise!

### quite a (meaning “really/very”)
- That’s **quite a** problem.

Note: these are common places where learners forget **a/an**:
- ❌ She’s such good teacher. → ✅ She’s such **a** good teacher.

---

## Common mistakes

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Why |
|---|---|---|
| I have few time. | I have little time. | time is uncountable |
| There is a few water. | There is a little water. | water is uncountable |
| She’s such good friend. | She’s such **a** good friend. | singular count noun |
| What mess! | What **a** mess! | countable “mess” |

## Practice

### A) Choose the best option
1) I have (a few / few) questions.
2) I have (a little / little) money.
3) There are (a few / few) people in the room, so it feels empty.
4) There is (a little / little) sugar left, but not much.
5) She’s such ___ interesting person.
6) What ___ beautiful day!

### B) Rewrite to match the meaning
7) Make it positive: “I have few friends here.”
8) Make it more negative: “There is a little time.”

---

## Answer key

1) a few  
2) a little  
3) few  
4) little  
5) an  
6) a  

7) I have **a few** friends here.  
8) There is **little** time.

---

## C1+ Notes

### Few/little can sound formal
In everyday speech, learners often prefer:
- not many (instead of few)
- not much (instead of little)

But few/little are still very useful in writing.
